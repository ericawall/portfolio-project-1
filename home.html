History of the festival

First festival 1999

The very first amateur sculpture festival was staged in 1999, and was co-ordinated by Brendan Doyle of the Cockleshell Art Gallery. Brendan had first seen sandsculpture the previous year on holiday in italy, and noticed that the sand used was very similar to the sand on Duncannon Beach.
image right: the late Brennie Power

First Artists

The gallery commissioned artists Daniel Doyle and Fergus Mulvaney to come and test the sand for suitability and to act as resident artists for the duration of the festival. Both artists were studying sculpture at D.I.T. at this time, and also members of the Irish Sand & Snow Sculpture collective.

Doyle & Mulvaney

Doyle and Mulvaney spent four days on the beach working on their centrepiece of work and simultaneously teaching and advising interested competitors on how to work with sand.
image right: Fergus Mulvaney working on sculpture

Performance Art

This aspect of the festival can be categorised as a "performance Art", as not only is a sculpture being exhibited, but the public can see how the sculpture is being made whilst also getting involved themselves.

"Shipwreck" Sculpture

Doyle and Mulvaney piece was called "Shipwreck" which featured a sailor clinging to a ships mast, hauling another another out of the sea. It measures approx. 6ft in height, with base measurements of 10ft x 15ft

Vandalism

The "Shipwreck" piece was originally 8ft in height, but had been vandalised on the first night of the festival. This action prompted all future exhibition pieces in subsequent festivals to be enclosed in a tent.
image left: "Shipwreck" sand sculpture

Amateur Competition

First place in the Amateur Competition this first year went to Danny Brennan, a local artist, with his sculpture of the celtic god of the sea, "Manannan Mac Lir".
image left: "Manannan Mac Lir" sand sculpture

Sculpture Measurements

It featured the head and hand of the god, gasping for air as he rises from the sea. His piece was finished minutes before the deadline, which was when the tide would come in. Brennan had never made a sandsculpture before.

2000 Festival

Following the successful first year, Cockleshell Gallery discovered at this time that Ireland was one of the few European countries without a national sandsculpting competition. Encouraged by the first festival's success, they began to pursue the task of organising and hosting an international Competition which would be Ireland's first Open Sand Sculpture Championship, as part of the nationwide Year 2000 Millenium Celebration Arts Program.

Theme "Sea Creatures"

The theme for 2000 was "Sea-Tales". The new artists involved were Neils "Edwin" Vegter from Holland, Caroline Saint-Olive from France, Nuria Villaginis from Spain and Sissa Yorgensen from Denmark. Other artists such as Niall Magee and Alan Magee have since joined this group.
image right: "Viking Sea-Worm" by Sissa & Fergus Mulvaney, 2000 festival
